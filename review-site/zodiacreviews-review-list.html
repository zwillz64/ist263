  • Goodfellas (1990)

    R, 146 mins. Directed by Martin Scorsese.
    Starring Robert De Niro, Ray Liotta, Joe Pesci

    My all-time favorite movie. So many iconic shots, so many badass one liners. Such career defining moments for the cast and crew. Scorsese nails the setting and gangster lifestyles perfectly, both positive and negative.
    It’s so much fun to watch the rise and fall of a man swallowed up by criminal conspiracy as the fellow Goodfellas he so easily befriended start dropping off one by one. You can’t get much better than this folks.


  • The Shawshank Redemption (1994)

    R, 142 mins. Directed by Frank Darabont.
    Starring Morgan Freeman, Tim Robbins, Bob Gunton

    One of my favorite films of all time. Completely engrossing and thought provoking film about the closest, most loyal group of prisoners you’ve ever seen.
    Everyone brings their A-game here: Director Frank Darabont’s beautiful adaptation of Stephen King’s novella, legendary cinematographer Roger Deakins’ sweeping shots of the prison, Thomas Newman’s music (especially the song introducing Shawshank), the entire cast (Morgan Freeman and Bob Gunton especially). It all comes together in an iconic climax that earns its satisfying ending. There’s so much to love about this movie and there’s a reason it’s considered a modern classic.


  • Parasite (2019)

    R, 132 mins. Directed by Bong Joon Ho.
    Starring Song Kang-Ho, Lee Sun-Kyun, Cho Yeo-Jeong

    Extremely glad this won Best Picture, being the first international film to win the award in America. Director Bong Joon-Ho makes a funny, disturbing, biting take on classism and the human condition. Park So-Dam and Song Kang-Ho play their roles with so much joy it sucks you in almost immediately.
    Watching this in the theater was way too much fun, before the halfway point turned into a tense thriller and the ending a chaotic finish. Had no idea where this film would take me next, but I’m glad I got on the ride.


  • Spider-Man: Into the Spider-Verse (2018)

    PG, 117 mins. Directed by Bob Perschietti, Peter Ramsey, & Rodney Rothman.
    Starring Shameik Moore, Hailee Steinfeld, Jake Johnson

    “It’s so dense, every single image has so many things going on” except that the movie we got was actually good.
    I’ve been a fan of Spider-Man for a long time, and when I learned about a Black-Puerto Rican Spider-Man from Donald Glover, I was way too excited. To finally see Miles Morales on the big screen was one of the best things I’ve gotten to experience. The story was unique to the other superhero movies coming out around the same time, it was very funny throughout, and the varied cast and gorgeous comic-themed animation made every scene pop.
    The scene just before the climax of the film had me tearing up in joy. The mix of gorgeous cinematography and motivational soundtrack was too much for me to handle in the theatre. Never did I think a Spider-Man film could top the second Raimi movie. This beats it by a long shot.


  • It's a Wonderful Life (1946)

    PG, 130 mins. Directed by Frank Capra.
    Starring James Stewart, Donna Reed, Lionel Barrymore

    2 things: Who was the moron who put Uncle Billy in charge of finances when he’s so scatterbrained he does the string on finger trick for 20 years straight?
    Also you’d think George would’ve caught the “never been born” thing much earlier after the 6th person he personally knew suddenly treated him like garbage.
    Regardless, there’s a reason why this film is considered a modern classic and a tradition to watch with the family every Christmas.


  • Hereditary (2018)

    R, 127 mins. Directed by Ari Aster.
    Starring Toni Collette, Milly Shapiro, Alex Wolff

    My personal best horror film of the decade and it earns this through so many ways:
    The cast is 100% excellent and has me confidently boarding the Toni Collette hype train. She was ROBBED of an Oscar nomination AND a win. Alex Wolff pulls off a realistically tormented teen filled with guilt and helplessness. Gabriel Byrne gives an understated and subtle role of the skeptic in the family. Milly Shapiro does a great job playing the “problem child”.
    The effects are disgustingly good, and the dinner scene is one of the best put to film. The build-up is slow but intriguing and carries needed tension into a fantastic, terrifying finale. I almost couldn’t sleep the night I saw this. And the more I watch it, the more I catch something new that adds to the film’s quality. iv. Please please PLEASE watch this, but don’t expect your average horror film.


  • Knives Out (2019)

    PG13, 130 mins. Directed by Rian Johnson.
    Starring Daniel Craig, Ana de Armas, Chris Evans

    You can tell that nearly every actor was having a blast making this movie, with special regards to Daniel Craig, Toni Collette, and Chris Evans. The script is sharp, funny, and surprisingly accurate to present-day politics and culture. The reveal of the mystery was smart and satisfying.


  • Judas and the Black Messiah

    R, 126 mins. Directed by Shaka King.
    Starring Daniel Kaluuya, Lakeith Stanfield, Jesse Plemons

    An absolutely heartbreaking and frustrating showcase of justified black nationalism demonized and propagandized by corrupt white men in power. The scary part is knowing Bill O’Neal (Stanfield) wasn’t the only vessel they used to collapse the party. Fred Hampton’s words still ring true to this day and I’m thankful they’re being carried out by his family and this film.


  • Borat Subsequent Moviefilm

    R, 95 mins. Directed by Jason Woliner.
    Starring Sacha Baron Cohen, Maria Bakalova

    Sacha Baron Cohen, with hilarious and convincing newcomer Maria Bakalova, continues to push the boundaries of social and cultural norms as his trademark foreigner character, although some of what used to be downright shocking 14 years ago has become our new normal. It isn’t quite as fresh as the original Borat, but still packs plenty of wild situations to make it worth watching. You still gain a sense of dread watching what horrible things our two leads will say and do to a real crowd that is unaware of the whole joke.
    I will say I was surprised by how well the film recovered in the wake of the ongoing pandemic and how it weaved itself into the film plot. But the debutante ball scene will haunt my dreams forever.


  • Big Fat Liar (2002)

    PG, 88 mins. Directed by Shawn Levy.
    Starring Frankie Muniz, Amanda Bynes, Paul Giamatti

    My absolute guiltiest of pleasures. Paul Giamatti was clearly having the time of his life while on set. The script is pretty dumb and some of the overacting can get tiresome, but the premise is just so funny. Who doesn’t love a well-deserved revenge plot? Its plot structure is so early ‘00s and I adore it for that.


  • Cruel Intentions (1999)

    R, 97 mins. Directed by Roger Kumble.
    Starring Ryan Phillippe, Sarah Michelle Gellar, Reese Witherspoon

    I couldn’t get into the movie because I had a hard time believing a preppy jerk would commit such an abrupt about-face simply because he met a nice, pretty girl at school. However, Sarah Michelle Gellar is delightfully "cruel" in her performance, and the soundtrack, especially “Colorblind”, works in the movie’s favor.


  • The Master of Disguise (2002)

    PG, 80 mins. Directed by Perry Andelin Blake.
    Starring Dana Carvey, Jennifer Esposito, Brent Spiner

    I loved this as a child, but I thoroughly dislike this as an adult. The dated (and pretty offensive) jokes don’t land, except for the recurring fart joke. And when the funniest parts of your movie are fart jokes, that indicates a pretty bad movie. This film is also super short, like they ran out of steam around the 60 minute mark. One third of it is outtakes and they're not even especially good outtakes.


  • Crash (2004)

    R, 112 mins. Directed by Paul Haggis.
    Starring Don Cheadle, Sandra Bullock, Ludacris

    Honestly, I am baffled that this film won the Oscar for Best Picture despite ruining its subject matter to this degree. There’s an interesting and thought provoking message buried under the uncomfortable and unrealistic racist behavior scattered throughout. The best-handled subplot in this film is the one with Michael Peña and his daughter, but the others come off as manipulative. The racism comparison scene in Do the Right Thing (1989) pulls off this idea significantly better.


  • Artemis Fowl (2020)

    PG, 95 mins. Directed by Kenneth Branagh.
    Starring Ferdia Shaw, Lara McDonnell, Josh Gad

    I am biased because I grew up reading the series this film is based on. I have never seen an adaptation quite so muddled, ugly, unfunny, inaccurate, and completely unearning of its title or final line. Who knows what great scenes we could have enjoyed instead of a Harry Potter knockoff lead and story changes that actually destroy the arcs of some of its characters?
    This was supposed to be a start to a movie franchise? Good luck with that. Stick to the novels, written or graphic. It’s a better use of your time.


  • The Last Airbender (2010)

    PG, 103 mins. Directed by M. Night Shyamalan.
    Starring Noah Ringer, Nicola Peltz, Jackson Rathbone

    “Look what they did to my boy.”
    What was M. Night Shyamalan thinking with this adaptation? None of the charm, humor, energy, vibrant colors, or enjoyable characters from the original acclaimed cartoon are reflected in this production. Not even the element bending is represented properly in a film that feels rushed, because it is rushing through the 20 episodes that make up the first season of the series.
    I originally thought Netflix couldn’t do worse than this, but the more I hear about it, the more it reminds me of this train wreck. Possibly the biggest disappointment of my childhood.


  • Transformers: Age of Extinction (2014)

    PG-13, 165 mins. Directed by Michael Bay.
    Starring Mark Wahlberg, Jack Reynor, TJ Miller

    This is one of the worst I’ve ever seen. My sister and I honestly had trouble finishing it.
    The script is crap, the acting is crap, the directing is crap. Optimus Prime completely stops caring about helping the humans, betraying his loyal character. The film switches locations so abruptly that it’s like you walked into a different movie. The only good part of this is Stanley Tucci, who’s barely trying. I had no intention of watching any additional sequels after I saw this train wreck (Bumblebee did turn out surprisingly well, though).


  • Wish Upon (2017)

    PG-13, 90 mins. Directed by John R. Leonetti.
    Starring Joey King, Ki Hong Lee, Ryan Phillippe

    Wow. This is really bad. The cast is fine in other things, but they do nothing for me here. The script is nonsensical and cliché, the kills are cheap and hilarious rather than scary, and the main character is so unbelievably stupid that you as a viewer are waiting for her to die. The best use for this film is for a drinking game with your friends.
